What I Do
I specialize in composing melodic, narrative-driven and what I call "active" music in the style of classic JRPGs such as Final Fantasy, the Chrono series, Tales series and those like them. What made these games special wasn’t just that their music was good; their music was a pillar of their worlds and stories.

That said, music can take either a passive or active role in world-building and storytelling. Passive music, like the kind you find in many films (and is increasingly prevalant in games), suits the scene very well, but is quickly forgotten; it describes moments as they happen. Active music, on the other hand, paints a vivid mental picture for the listener and excites their imagination; it tells stories. This is the kind of music that carved the legacies of these classic series and fully realized worlds, characters and stories with only 16-bit bardware.

My bottom line isn't just to make fitting but quickly-forgotten background filler, but rather to make music that is a selling point and integral part of the identity of the games I work on. Good-enough music is easy to come by, but music that drives players to YouTube anywhere from days to years later to relive the game and their memories with its music is a good bit trickier to find.

It's said that true joy is composed of a tear and a smile, and when that "YouTube" day comes for someone, anything less won't do.

A: These are just terms I use to describe the differences I perceive among different kinds of music. Very generally speaking, "passive" music is the kind you often hear in films: fitting, but a bit forgettable because it tends to blend into the backdrop unnoticed. In a sense, passive music is like a literal account of facts while "active" music, on the other hand, is more like telling a story around a campfire.

Put another way, you could say that passive music makes little sense by itself, or that it seems incomplete out of its original context, while active music can stand on its own and paint a vivid mental picture even out of context, hence the terms "passive" and "active," respectively.

In a word, passive music follows moments as they happen; active music tells stories. How a composer structures their music determines which category it falls into.

My name’s Geoff Butterworth, also known as Vermanubis, and it’s a pleasure to meet you.

Game music has been my life since I was 5 years old when I would do 6-hour marathons through my corrupted copy of Final Fantasy: Mystic Quest just to hear the final dungeon theme. Around my late teens, I finally realized that when you feel as strongly about something as I did (and do) about game music, you make it your purpose in life.

Since about that age it's been my life's work to get others to feel what my most beloved games made me feel through my own work. If you can see that fact reflected in my music and want music that will cast your story and world in stone, it's hard to overstate what a pleasure it'd be to help you make a game that'll mean as much to others as my favorite games mean to me.
