What I Do
I think game music is at its best when it’s just as fun to listen to as it is beautiful. It’s precisely the combination of beauty and enthusiasm of the music of games like Final Fantasy, Castlevania or Chrono Trigger that won them the hearts of almost everyone who played them, and drives those players to YouTube almost 30 years later to hear that music.

That said, my bottom line is to make music that follows in that tradition: to write compellingly beautiful music without sacrificing a sincere sense of fun and enthusiasm. Beautiful music is easy to come by, but music that drives players to YouTube anywhere from days to decades later to relive worlds and stories through their music is much trickier to find.

It's said that true joy is composed of a tear and a smile, and when that "YouTube" day comes for someone, anything less won't do.

A: These are just terms I use to describe the differences I perceive among different kinds of music. Very generally speaking, "passive" music is the kind you often hear in films: fitting, but a bit forgettable because it tends to blend into the backdrop unnoticed. In a sense, passive music is like a literal account of facts while "active" music, on the other hand, is more like telling a story around a campfire. Facts are informative, but stories give a sense of scope and meaning.

Put another way, you could say that passive music makes little sense by itself, or that it seems incomplete out of its original context, while active music can stand on its own and paint a vivid mental picture even out of context, hence the terms "passive" and "active," respectively.

In a word, passive music follows moments as they happen; active music tells stories. How a composer structures their music determines which category it falls into.

My name’s Geoff Butterworth, also known as Vermanubis, and it’s a pleasure to meet you.

Game music has been my life since I was 5 years old when I would do 6-hour marathons through my corrupted copy of Final Fantasy: Mystic Quest just to hear the final dungeon theme. Around my late teens, I finally realized that when you feel as strongly about something as I did (and do) about game music, you dedicate yourself wholly to it.

Since about that age it's been my life's work to communicate my most beloved memories and sentiments from games to others through my own work.
